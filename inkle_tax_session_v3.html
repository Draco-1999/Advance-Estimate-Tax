01

The IRS Mindset

Why does any of this exist?

The Foundation

The IRS is not a villain. It's just impatient.

The US government runs on tax money — roads, military, social security, the FDA checking your food. But tax returns are filed in April. Once a year. The government can't wait a whole year to pay its bills.

So the IRS built a rule: if you're going to owe tax, pay it as you earn it. Throughout the year. Not in one April lump sum. This is the Pay As You Go (PAYG) principle, and everything in estimated taxes flows from it.

💡 Employees vs. businesses: Employees have tax withheld from every paycheck automatically — they never think about this. Businesses? No one withholds anything. The company keeps all its money until it pays the IRS. Estimated taxes are the fix.
🍕
The Pizza Shop Analogy
Why paying once a year is a problem

You open a pizza shop. All year: $200K revenue, $150K expenses, $50K profit. You spend your cash on equipment, salaries, rent. You don't think about taxes.

Then April arrives. Your accountant says: "You owe $10,500." Where does that come from? You've already spent it.

The IRS invented estimated taxes precisely to prevent this scenario. Pay $2,625 every quarter. Nobody gets a shock. Cash flows steadily to the government. Everyone goes home happy.

The magic number: $500

The rule kicks in only if your company expects to owe $500 or more in federal income tax for the year (§6655). Below that — no payments required, no penalty.

02

The Four Dates

When exactly does money move?

Four times a year. Same dates. Every year.

For a calendar-year corporation (most of our clients), the due dates are fixed. Miss them → underpayment penalty. Know them → avoid it.

Q1
April 15 — First installment
Covers Jan–Mar income. Same day as individual returns — easy to remember. 25% of annual estimate due.
Q2
June 16 — Second installment
Cumulative 50% of the full annual estimate must be paid by this date (Q1 + Q2 together). Q2 covers only 2 months but the cumulative bar is 50%. Feels harsh. It is.
Q3
September 15 — Third installment
Cumulative 75% of annual estimate paid. Covers June–August income.
Q4
December 15 — Final installment
C-corps pay in December, not January. 100% of annual estimate should be paid. Don't confuse with the individual January 15 deadline.
⚠️ The Q2 trap everyone falls into: Q2 only covers April–May (2 months) but the cumulative payment due is 50% of the whole year. The IRS designed it this way. Just accept it and build it into your workflow.
What about fiscal year clients (e.g. FYE March 31)?›
Fiscal year companies have shifted due dates. Q1 is due on the 15th of the 4th month of their fiscal year. FYE March 31 → Q1 due July 15, Q2 due Sep 16, Q3 due Dec 15, Q4 due Mar 15. Always check the FYE before using any due date.
Can you skip a quarter if cash is tight?›
Technically yes — but the IRS charges an underpayment penalty for missed quarters. The penalty compounds daily using the federal short-term rate + 3%. It's usually small but completely avoidable. Better to pay and not wonder.

03

The Calculation

Two methods. One safe. One accurate.

The IRS doesn't want perfection. It wants effort.

You don't need to know exactly what a company will earn this year. You just need to pick the right method and calculate reasonably. Two options:

MethodFormulaWhen to usePenalty risk
Prior Year Safe HarborLast year's tax ÷ 4 per quarterCompany paid tax last yearZero — IRS can't touch you
Current Year ProjectionProjected this year's tax × 25%No prior year, or prior year = $0Low if projections are honest
✅ Always try safe harbor first. It's the fastest, safest, and requires zero guessing about the current year. Only switch to projection when there's literally no prior year base.

04

Book vs. Tax — The Adjustments

Why the IRS sees a completely different number

The Most Important Chapter

Your books say one thing. The IRS calculates something different.

This is the part that surprises people most — even experienced ones. A company's accounting books show a profit or loss. But the IRS does not tax accounting profit. It taxes taxable income, calculated by its own rules.

The difference between what your books show and what the IRS taxes is made up of book-to-tax adjustments. Some add income back in. Some reduce it. Here are the main ones you'll encounter:

💻
§174 R&E Costs
Offshore dev spend expensed on books but must be amortized over 15 years for IRS. Huge add-back.
Adds to income
🍽️
Meals & Entertainment
Only 50% of business meals are deductible for tax. If books show 100%, the other 50% gets added back.
Adds to income
🚫
Discontinued / One-Time Expenses
Severance, shutdown costs, legal settlements — these may hit the books but have special IRS treatment.
Varies
📉
Depreciation Differences
Tax depreciation (MACRS) often differs from book depreciation. Can add or reduce taxable income.
Can go either way
🔁
NOL Carryforward
Past losses carried forward — reduces taxable income up to 80% of current year. Covered in Ch 5.
Reduces income
💰
Stock-Based Compensation
Treated differently in books vs. tax returns. Can reduce taxable income significantly for tech startups.
Often reduces income
📌 Key mindset: Never look at a company's P&L and assume that's what the IRS taxes. Always ask: what adjustments apply? The two numbers can be wildly different — especially for our clients.

Meals & Entertainment — the 50% rule everyone forgets

A company spends $40,000 on client dinners, team lunches, and entertainment this year. On the books: $40,000 expense. Looks fully deducted. But the IRS says only 50% of business meals are deductible.

🍽️
MealTime — meals & entertainment add-back
Simple but commonly missed
Meals & entertainment per books$40,000
IRS-allowed deduction (50%)$20,000
Add-back to taxable income+ $20,000

It's small compared to §174 — but it's real, it's consistent, and it's commonly overlooked. Always check the meals line.

Discontinued expenses — when a shutdown creates a surprise

Sometimes a company shuts down a product line, lays off a team, or settles a legal dispute. These create large one-time expenses on the books. The tax treatment depends on what exactly happened — but they always need to be checked.

🚫
Vertix Corp — shutting down a division
One-time discontinued expenses

Vertix shut down its hardware division in Q1. They recorded $200,000 in severance, asset write-downs, and contract termination fees. Their books show a big loss.

The IRS treats these differently: some portions are deductible in the current year, others must be capitalized or spread. The net result is their taxable income is higher than the book loss suggests — even before §174.

⚠️ When you see one-time or discontinuation charges on a P&L — flag them. Don't assume they're fully deductible. Always escalate to tax review before finalizing the estimate.

05

Section 174 — The Big One

Offshore dev spend and the 15-year amortization trap

The Offshore R&E Bomb

A startup with a book loss of $200K can owe $19,950 by June 16.

This is the most important thing in this entire session. Read it slowly.

The IRS says: if your company pays people to create software, conduct research, or develop technology — that spend creates intellectual property, something of lasting value. You can't deduct it all in one year. You have to spread it out.

For offshore work: spread over 15 years, with a mid-year convention. For US-based work: immediate full deduction (restored by the OBBBA). The gap between these two is enormous.

🚨 §174 foreign R&E: Offshore R&D costs → capitalized → amortized over 15 years → mid-year convention (÷ 2 in Year 1). Only 1/30th deductible in Year 1. Everything else → added back to income.

Why ÷ 15 and then ÷ 2?

÷ 15: The IRS assigns a 15-year useful life to foreign R&E. So you spread the cost over 15 years.
÷ 2: The "mid-year convention" — the IRS assumes you started the work on July 1 of the year, regardless of when you actually started. So Year 1 = only half a year of amortization.

Together: $600,000 in offshore dev → only $600,000 ÷ 15 ÷ 2 = $20,000 deductible in Year 1. The other $580,000 gets added back to taxable income.

Location of work§174 ruleYear 1 deduction on $600K
🇺🇸 US-basedImmediate full deduction$600,000
🌍 Offshore (India, Eastern Europe, etc.)15-year amortization~$20,000

Click or tap to flip

Does §174 apply to SaaS companies?
Yes. Software = R&E in IRS eyes. Any offshore product dev, backend engineering, or infra work falls under §174 regardless of what you call it.
US invoice, but offshore work — which rule applies?
Foreign R&E. What matters is where the work is performed, not where the invoice comes from. Code written in Chennai = foreign R&E, even if billed through a US agency.
What happens in Years 2–15?
From Year 2 to Year 15 you deduct the full annual amount ($600K ÷ 15 = $40K/yr). Year 16 gets the final half-year ($20K). Total across all years = $600K exactly.
Domestic R&E after the OBBBA?
Fully deductible immediately again — the OBBBA restored this. So a startup with a US dev team pays no §174 penalty. Only offshore work still faces 15-year amortization.
Can a company argue its offshore spend isn't R&E?
Only if the work is clearly routine maintenance or non-experimental. Building a product or technology? The IRS almost always calls it R&E. Don't push this without a tax attorney.
What's the formula to memorize?
Year 1 deduction = Offshore spend ÷ 15 ÷ 2. Add-back = Offshore spend − Year 1 deduction. Add-back flips the book income to taxable income.

06

NOL Carryforwards

Using past losses to cut today's tax bill

The Tax Voucher

Lost money in a previous year? The IRS gives you a voucher.

When a company has a net operating loss — it lost more money than it made in a year, after all IRS adjustments — the IRS says: "We can't take tax from you this year. But hold onto that loss — use it to reduce taxes in a future profitable year."

This is a Net Operating Loss (NOL) carryforward. It doesn't disappear. It accumulates year over year until the company can use it.

📌 Most seed-stage startups have NOL carryforwards. They lose money for 1–3 years before becoming profitable. That pile of accumulated losses is a real, valuable tax asset — always check for it.

The 80% cap — the one rule you must memorize

Here's the catch: the IRS doesn't let you use your full NOL to zero out all taxable income. Under TCJA rules (post-2017 losses), you can offset maximum 80% of current year taxable income. The remaining 20% is always taxable.

NOL — tap to flip

Click or tap to reveal

Do post-2017 NOLs expire?
No — they carry forward indefinitely. Pre-2018 NOLs had a 20-year limit AND could offset 100% of income (no 80% cap). Most of our startup clients have post-2017 losses.
Which comes first — §174 or NOL?
§174 first. Always calculate taxable income with all add-backs first. THEN apply the 80% NOL cap to that final taxable income number. Order is non-negotiable.
Where do you find the NOL on a return?
Form 1120, Line 29a shows the NOL deduction taken. Schedule K or attached schedules show the full carryforward balance. Prior returns may have carryforward worksheets.
Can an NOL ever fully eliminate estimated taxes?
Rarely. Even a massive NOL can only offset 80% of taxable income. The remaining 20% is always taxable. And if §174 generated the taxable income — it's usually a large enough number that 20% still matters.

The Inkle Workflow

Six steps, every client, no exceptions

This is the process — internalize it.

1
Pull the prior year Form 1120 — find Line 31
That's the tax liability. Your safe harbor base. If it's $0 or doesn't exist (first-year corp), skip to Step 3.
2
Check for NOL carryforward
Schedule K or prior returns. Note the amount. Remember: 80% cap applies. Carry it into your projection.
3
Review the current year P&L for adjustments
Look for: offshore contractor/dev spend → §174. Meals & entertainment → 50% disallowed. One-time/discontinued charges → flag for review. Stock compensation → check treatment.
4
Run all applicable add-backs
§174: offshore spend ÷ 15 ÷ 2 = allowed. Rest = add-back. Meals: 50% of total = add-back. Then apply 80% NOL cap to the resulting taxable income.
5
Choose the method and calculate
Safe harbor available? Line 31 ÷ 4. Not available? (Taxable income × 21%) × 50% cumulative − Q1 already paid.
6
Flag, escalate if needed, track everything
Anything unclear (new product line, unusual charges, unclassified contractors)? Flag it. Don't guess. Escalate to tax review. Update the eight-column tracker.

Flash cards — test yourself

Tap each card to see the answer

What's the $500 rule exactly?
§6655: If a corporation expects to owe $500+ in federal tax, quarterly estimated payments are required. Below $500 = no payments, no penalty.
What does safe harbor actually protect against?
The underpayment penalty. Pay last year's full tax ÷ 4 each quarter and the IRS cannot charge you a penalty — even if you end up owing much more.
How much of a meal expense can you deduct?
50%. The other 50% must be added back to taxable income. This applies to business meals, client dinners, team lunches — anything in the meals & entertainment category.
What is a book-to-tax adjustment?
The difference between what a company shows as profit/loss in its accounting books versus what the IRS actually taxes. §174 add-backs and meals disallowances are the main ones for our clients.
What Form 1120 line number is the safe harbor base?
Line 31 — "Total tax." Divide by 4 to get each quarterly safe harbor payment. First stop for every client estimate.
Why December 15 for Q4, not January?
C-corps have a different Q4 deadline than individuals. Individuals pay January 15. Corporations pay December 15 of the same year. Easy to mix up — always double-check.
